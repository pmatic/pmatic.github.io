Museums - Places of Dialogue and Encounter

Technical Museum Nikola Tesla, Zagreb, 2019

In 2019, the project Nikola Tesla - Migrant expanded into a collaboration with the Ethnographic Museum and the Typhlological Museum. The collaboration, named “Museums as Places of Dialogue and Encounter”, featured a workshop for teenagers, including young people with experience of forced migration, and was financially supported by the City of Zagreb. During the project planning phase, the project team wanted to visit Zagreb Asylum Centre Porin. Unfortunately, the project's partners, Are You Syrious, and the Centre for Peace Studies did not manage to prolong their contract with the Ministry of the Interior, and the plans for the visit had to be given up.

Another initial project idea was to invite the German artists, Andreas Maus and Karmen Frankl and organize a workshop and a film showing. As part of the initiative “Keupstrasse is Everywhere”, a community project that emerged as a reaction to the case of neo-Nazi bombings that exposed police connections to the terrorist groups in Cologne in 2014, Frankl organizes workshops with police officers on the topic of neo-Nazi terrorism and protection of affected people; Maus is the author of the documentary. Soon after deciding on the collaboration, the Croatian Ombudswoman published an anonymous letter of Croatian police offers, who sought her help as they were obliged by their superiors to use violence on the Croatian-Bosnian border (Ombudswoman 2019).

After consideration, the project team decided that organizing a workshop on police violence in a time of heightened tensions between state officials and police offers could cause damage to the project. The team gave up on the idea of engaging with the police and decided to make a workshop for children, including children with experiences of migration.

During the workshop preparation, the team visited Zagreb's Children Correctional Institution, where unaccompanied asylum seeker minors are often placed, with the idea to involve them in the workshop. The Institution's staff stated that they had no children who would be interested in the workshop and that their English skills were lacking.

Inclusion of children with migrant experience turned out to be very demanding. The workshop happened on a weekday, not long after a general strike of education unions, and plenty of children with migration experience were busy catching up with schoolwork. They were not interested in missing another day of school, as they had enough problems already due to the language barrier.

Are You Syrious initiative tried to include seven unaccompanied children from Afghanistan and Vietnam who had recently arrived at Zagreb Asylum Center, but it turned out that the information about their guardians was inaccessible. Since it is impossible to have minors in a workshop without a guardian's consent, this idea was given up as well.

A few days before the workshop, a volunteer of the Centre for Peace Studies confirmed the participation of two youth with migration experience, a 19-year-old who was not in the education system, and an 11-year-old elementary school pupil. Even though the workshop targeted high schoolers, both kids accepted the invitation.

The workshop began with a game of greetings which are characteristic of different cultures. Each participant received instructions on a way of greeting specific to a certain culture and was asked to greet all the persons in the room in that way. Since each participant had a certain way of greeting which differed from the others, this caused a lot of misunderstandings, different degrees of comfort in encounters, and laughter. This turned out to be a good ice-breaker method, and the workshop proceeded to the next activity, a conversation on cultural differences, personal space, and expressions of emotions and closeness.

A key part of the workshop included the method of emotional connecting through museum objects (Dibbits, Rana i Willemsen 2017). A yellow armband with three black dots from Zagreb’s Typhlological Museum's collection, which was used for people with vision impairment after the World War One, was passed around the group. The emotions that emerged were mostly negative. Several participants expressed that it reminded them of armbands that the Nazis used to mark the members of the Jewish community. The debate that developed from the conversation on the armband included topics such as differences, discrimination, disability, LGBTIQ, xenophobia, and racism. The local participants were significantly motivated and communicative, while the participants with migration experience were more reserved, which they explained by language difficulties. The workshop language changed several times from Croatian to English and back, and the final reflection concluded that subsequent workshops should be bilingual and use both Croatian and English so that everyone could participate.

By the end of the workshop, the project team informed the participants on the failed initiative of including the unaccompanied children. The participants reacted with anger and disbelief towards the injustice and demanded that the project team should work harder next time.

In the final reflection, the high schoolers concluded that the workshop was very interesting since they could communicate freely and meet new people, and they suggested that this kind of education should become mandatory for both elementary and high schools. The participants with migration background expressed sadness because they did not visit the whole museum except for one room where the workshop was held, but other than that, they were satisfied too.
